Non-biblical *reproduced quotations* are taken from **public-domain translations** (KJV is
no longer used now that the Bible text is NASB). Where no public-domain translation of a
passage was available (or where the text survives only in fragments), the card is marked
**"summary"** rather than **"quoted"** — a paraphrase with a citation, never an invented
quotation.

Each source card carries two dates, on purpose:

- **Tradition era** — when the story belongs / how old the oral tradition is.
- **Text recorded** — when the surviving manuscript was actually written.

This is why traditions like the Norse Eddas or the Maya *Popol Vuh* can sit in a book about
the pre-Roman world even though their *manuscripts* are medieval or post-contact: the
**ink is late, the tradition is ancient**, and the timeline view shows both honestly.

### Verbatim quotations used (verified against public-domain text)
- **Berossus / Xisuthrus** — "After the flood had abated, [Xisuthrus] sent out birds … they
  now returned with their feet tinged with mud. … they returned to him no more." — Cory's
  translation in Smith, *Chaldean Account of Genesis* (1876).
- **Genesis 7:19; 8:7–9; 8:4** — "And the waters prevailed exceedingly upon the earth …
  And the ark rested … upon the mountains of Ararat." — KJV (1611).
- **Ovid, *Metamorphoses* I** — "And now sea and land had no mark of distinction; everything
  now was ocean … When here Deucalion … borne in a little ship … first rested." — Riley (1851).
- **Shatapatha Brahmana I.8.1** — "A flood will carry away all these creatures: from that I
  will save thee! … by preparing a ship … I will save thee from it." — Eggeling (1882).
- **Mencius III** — "In the time of Yao, the waters, flowing out of their channels,
  inundated the Middle Kingdom." — Legge.

## Licensing note
Reproduced quotations are public domain. **Modern copyrighted translations** (e.g. Andrew
George's Gilgamesh, Dennis Tedlock's *Popol Vuh*) are *excellent* and worth owning, but are
only **linked or paraphrased here, never reproduced**, so the site stays freely shareable.

## Scope rule
Following the project's intent, content is restricted to traditions describing the ancient /
origin world whose roots predate the fall of Rome (c. 400 CE), even where the surviving
manuscript is later. Each card states its manuscript date explicitly.
